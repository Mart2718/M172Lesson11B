7 · Coterminal Angles

Definition — OpenStax §7.1

Coterminal Angles: two angles in standard position are coterminal if they share the same initial and terminal side — they point in the same direction but may have completed a different number of full rotations.

To find a coterminal angle: add or subtract multiples of \(360^\circ\) (in degrees) or \(2\pi\) (in radians).

\[\theta \pm n\cdot 360^\circ \quad\text{or}\quad \theta \pm n\cdot 2\pi\]

Predict: which is coterminal with \(45^\circ\) — \(405^\circ\), \(315^\circ\), or \(-45^\circ\)?


Answer: \(405^\circ = 45^\circ + 360^\circ\), so it is coterminal. \(315^\circ\) and \(-45^\circ\) are not (they're coterminal with each other but not with \(45^\circ\)).

Worked Example 1 — Reduce \(7058^\circ\)

Find an angle in \([0^\circ, 360^\circ)\) coterminal with \(7058^\circ\).

Step 1: Divide: \(7058 \div 360 \approx 19.6\). So 19 full turns are inside \(7058^\circ\).
Step 2: Subtract \(19 \cdot 360 = 6840\). \(\;7058 - 6840 = 218\).
Coterminal angle: \(218^\circ\) (in QIII).

Worked Example 2 — Reduce \(31\pi/6\)

Step 1: Subtract \(2\pi=\dfrac{12\pi}{6}\) until inside \([0,2\pi)\).
Step 2: \(\dfrac{31\pi}{6}-\dfrac{12\pi}{6}=\dfrac{19\pi}{6}\); again \(\dfrac{19\pi}{6}-\dfrac{12\pi}{6}=\dfrac{7\pi}{6}\).
Coterminal: \(\dfrac{7\pi}{6}\) (QIII, ref angle \(30^\circ\)).

8 · All 6 Trig Ratios from a Point on the Terminal Side

Definition — OpenStax §7.4 / SAC Notes

Key Idea: if a point \((x,y)\) lies on the terminal side of \(\theta\), the distance from the origin acts as the hypotenuse: \(r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\). All 6 ratios follow without needing the angle.

\[\sin\theta=\dfrac{y}{r},\ \cos\theta=\dfrac{x}{r},\ \tan\theta=\dfrac{y}{x}\]

\[\csc\theta=\dfrac{r}{y},\ \sec\theta=\dfrac{r}{x},\ \cot\theta=\dfrac{x}{y}\]

Signs: \(r\) is always positive. The signs of \(x\) and \(y\) determine the signs of the ratios — no need to memorize ASTC separately when you use \((x,y)\) directly.

Worked Example 3 — Point \((-5, 12)\)

Find all 6 trig ratios for \(\theta\) with terminal side through \((-5,12)\).

Step 1: \(r=\sqrt{(-5)^2+12^2}=\sqrt{169}=13\).
Step 2: Quadrant: \(x<0, y>0\Rightarrow\) QII.
Step 3: Apply definitions.
\(\sin=\dfrac{12}{13},\ \cos=-\dfrac{5}{13},\ \tan=-\dfrac{12}{5}\)
\(\csc=\dfrac{13}{12},\ \sec=-\dfrac{13}{5},\ \cot=-\dfrac{5}{12}\)

For point \((-48, 55)\), which quadrant is \(\theta\) in, and what is \(r\)?


Answer: \(x<0, y>0\Rightarrow\) QII. \(r=\sqrt{48^2+55^2}=\sqrt{2304+3025}=\sqrt{5329}=73\).

9 · All 6 Ratios from One Trig Value + Quadrant

Definition — OpenStax §7.4 / SAC Notes

Key Idea: if you know one ratio and the quadrant of \(\theta\), find the rest using the Pythagorean Identity and ASTC.

Pythagorean Identity: \(\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1\).

Three steps: (1) substitute the known ratio to find the other; (2) use the quadrant to assign the correct sign; (3) compute the four reciprocals.

Worked Example 4 — \(\sin\theta=\dfrac{2}{3}\), QII

Step 1: \(\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta=1 \Rightarrow \dfrac{4}{9}+\cos^2\theta=1\Rightarrow \cos^2\theta=\dfrac{5}{9}\). Magnitude: \(\dfrac{\sqrt{5}}{3}\).
Step 2: QII: cosine is negative. So \(\cos\theta=-\dfrac{\sqrt{5}}{3}\).
Step 3: \(\tan = \dfrac{\sin}{\cos}=\dfrac{2/3}{-\sqrt{5}/3}=-\dfrac{2}{\sqrt{5}}=-\dfrac{2\sqrt{5}}{5}\). Reciprocals: \(\csc=\dfrac{3}{2},\ \sec=-\dfrac{3}{\sqrt{5}}=-\dfrac{3\sqrt{5}}{5},\ \cot=-\dfrac{\sqrt{5}}{2}\).
\(\sin=\dfrac{2}{3},\ \cos=-\dfrac{\sqrt{5}}{3},\ \tan=-\dfrac{2\sqrt{5}}{5},\ \csc=\dfrac{3}{2},\ \sec=-\dfrac{3\sqrt{5}}{5},\ \cot=-\dfrac{\sqrt{5}}{2}\)

10 · Trig Symmetry — Same Value, Different Angle

Definition — OpenStax §7.3

Big idea: trig values repeat. Different angles can share a sine or cosine because of the unit circle's symmetry.

Same Sine Rule: \(\sin\theta=\sin(180^\circ-\theta)\) — supplements share sine.

Same Cosine Rule: \(\cos\theta=\cos(360^\circ-\theta)\) — reflection across the x-axis shares cosine.

Coterminal connection: any \(\theta\pm 360^\circ\) (or \(\pm 2\pi\)) shares all six trig values.

Worked Example 5 — \(\tan(390^\circ)\)

Step 1: \(390^\circ - 360^\circ = 30^\circ\). Coterminal!
Step 2: \(\tan(30^\circ)=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}=\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\).
\(\tan(390^\circ)=\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\)

Worked Example 6 — Find an angle with the same sine as \(190^\circ\)

Step 1: \(190^\circ\) is in QIII. Reference angle \(=10^\circ\). \(\sin(190^\circ)\) is negative.
Step 2: Sine is negative in QIII and QIV. The other angle with reference \(10^\circ\) and negative sine is in QIV: \(360^\circ-10^\circ=350^\circ\).
\(\sin(190^\circ)=\sin(350^\circ)\)

Find another angle in \([0^\circ, 360^\circ)\) with the same cosine as \(100^\circ\).


Answer: \(\cos(100^\circ) = \cos(360^\circ - 100^\circ) = \cos(260^\circ)\). Both are in quadrants where cosine is negative (QII and QIII), with reference angle \(80^\circ\).

Q3 (short answer). In your own words, explain why two different angles can have the exact same sine.


Model: Sine is the y-coordinate on the unit circle. Two different angles can land on points with the same y-value — supplementary angles (\(\theta\) and \(180^\circ-\theta\)) reflect across the y-axis but keep the same height. Coterminal angles also share all trig values because they end at exactly the same point.
